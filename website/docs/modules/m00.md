# Module 00: Setup & Tools

In 2006, NASA needed an antenna for its Space Technology 5 mission. The requirements were brutal: it had to work across multiple frequencies, fit on a tiny satellite, and survive the vibrations of a rocket launch. Human engineers had spent months trying to design one. Nothing worked well enough.

So they did something unusual: they *evolved* one.[^1]

They started with a population of random antenna shapes — just bent wires, described as sequences of construction commands. They evaluated each antenna in a physics simulator. The best ones survived. The worst ones died. The survivors were mutated — a wire segment lengthened here, an angle changed there — and crossed with each other to produce offspring. After thousands of generations, what emerged looked like nothing any human would design: a bizarre, asymmetric, zigzagging structure that looked like a crumpled paperclip. But it met every specification, outperformed the human designs, and actually flew in space.

No engineer designed that antenna. Evolution did.

That's what this course is about: **Evolution Strategies** — algorithms that solve problems the way nature does, not through calculus and gradients, but through variation, selection, and the relentless pressure of "whatever works, works."

## Evolution is the Original Optimizer

Think about it. Biological evolution has "designed" every organism you've ever seen: the aerodynamics of a peregrine falcon (the fastest animal alive, diving at 240 mph), the sonar system of a bat (more precise than anything Boeing has built), the immune system (a parallel search algorithm that identifies and destroys novel threats it has never encountered before). No blueprints. No gradients. No loss function written on a whiteboard. Just mutation, selection, and time.

Evolution Strategies (ES) are computational algorithms inspired by this process. They're part of a broader family called **evolutionary computation**, and they have a remarkable property: they work on problems where gradient-based methods can't, won't, or shouldn't be used.

## Where ES Shines (and Why You Should Care)

If gradient descent is a GPS navigator — efficient but needs roads — then ES is an off-road vehicle. It's not always the fastest way to get somewhere, but it goes places the GPS can't:

- **OpenAI showed in 2017** that ES can train neural network policies for complex robotics tasks, matching or beating standard RL algorithms — and doing it 10x faster in wall-clock time because ES parallelizes trivially across hundreds of CPUs.[^2] No backpropagation. No replay buffers. No gradient computation. Just: "try a bunch of things, keep what works."

- **CMA-ES** (Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy) — the crown jewel of this course — is considered the gold standard for black-box continuous optimization.[^3] If you have a function you need to minimize and you can't (or don't want to) compute its gradient, CMA-ES is often the first thing experts reach for. It's been the dominant algorithm in the Black-Box Optimization Benchmarking (BBOB) competition for over a decade.[^4]

- **Neuroevolution** — evolving the architecture and weights of neural networks — produced NEAT,[^5] an algorithm that evolves both the topology and connection weights of a network simultaneously. NEAT agents have learned to play video games, control robots, and even compose music, all without backpropagation.

- **Quality-Diversity algorithms** like MAP-Elites[^6] don't just find one good solution — they fill an entire archive of diverse, high-performing solutions. Imagine a robot that has a repertoire of hundreds of different walking gaits, so when it loses a leg, it can instantly switch to a gait that still works. That's QD in action, and it's one of the most exciting frontiers in evolutionary computation.

- **Car design at BMW** uses evolutionary optimization to explore the design space of vehicle components — finding shapes that minimize drag while satisfying structural constraints that would make a gradient optimizer cry.[^7]

- **Drug design** uses evolutionary algorithms to explore the space of molecular structures, mutating and recombining candidate molecules to optimize binding affinity, toxicity, and synthesizability simultaneously.[^8]

- **Game AI**: Before deep RL took over, evolutionary methods were the dominant approach for game AI. The 2014 General Video Game AI competition was won by an evolutionary planning algorithm.[^9] And in some domains — especially where the reward landscape is deceptive or has lots of local optima — ES still wins.

:::tip When to use ES over gradient descent
Use gradient descent when you have a differentiable loss function and clean gradients. Use ES when any of these are true:
- The objective is **non-differentiable** (combinatorial problems, simulations with if-statements and discontinuities)
- The objective is **noisy** (real-world measurements, stochastic simulations)
- The objective is a **black box** (you can evaluate it but can't see inside it)
- You want **diverse solutions**, not just one optimum
- You need to optimize over **discrete structures** (network topologies, program syntax)
- The landscape has **many local optima** that trap gradient methods
:::

## A Brief History of Evolving Solutions

The idea of using evolution as a computational tool is older than you might think:

- **1960s**: Three groups independently invented evolutionary computation. Ingo Rechenberg and Hans-Paul Schwefel in Berlin developed **Evolution Strategies** to optimize the shapes of jet nozzles and bent pipes in wind tunnel experiments — literal hardware optimization where you couldn't compute a gradient if you wanted to.[^10] John Holland at the University of Michigan developed **Genetic Algorithms**.[^11] Lawrence Fogel in California developed **Evolutionary Programming**. None of them knew about the others at first.

- **1973**: Rechenberg published his foundational work on the (1+1)-ES — the simplest possible evolution strategy, with one parent and one offspring. He proved the **1/5th success rule**: if more than 1/5 of mutations are successful, increase the mutation step size; if fewer, decrease it. This was the first *adaptive* step-size control — the algorithm tuning itself as it runs. You'll implement this in Module 02.[^10]

- **1996**: Nikolaus Hansen and Andreas Ostermeier invented **CMA-ES**.[^3] This was a breakthrough: the algorithm learns not just the right step size but the right *shape* of the search distribution — it learns the correlations between variables. If your function has a narrow, rotated valley, CMA-ES will discover the rotation and adapt. This is Module 04, and it's where things get *really* good.

- **2002**: Kenneth Stanley published **NEAT** (NeuroEvolution of Augmenting Topologies).[^5] Instead of evolving just the weights of a fixed neural network, NEAT evolves the topology too — adding neurons and connections over time, starting from the simplest possible network. It's evolution operating on the structure of intelligence itself.

- **2015**: Mouret and Clune published **MAP-Elites**,[^6] launching the Quality-Diversity revolution. Instead of converging to a single optimum, MAP-Elites illuminates the entire space of possible solutions — showing you not just the best solution, but the best solution *for every possible combination of behavioral traits*.

- **2017**: Salimans et al. at OpenAI showed that a simple ES algorithm (not even CMA-ES, just basic Gaussian ES with a clever parallelization trick) could train deep neural network policies for MuJoCo locomotion tasks, competing with state-of-the-art RL algorithms.[^2] The deep learning community, which had spent years refining backpropagation, was stunned.

- **2022**: ES methods began appearing in large-scale hyperparameter optimization, neural architecture search, and even LLM fine-tuning — anywhere gradients are noisy, expensive, or unavailable.[^12]

The field has come a long way from Rechenberg's bent pipes in the wind tunnel.

## 3. The Evolution Loop — Simple, Powerful, Universal

Every evolutionary algorithm follows the same loop. It's been the same since Rechenberg in the 1960s:

```text
1. Create a population of candidate solutions (random at first)
2. Evaluate each candidate (compute fitness)
3. Select the best candidates
4. Create new candidates by mutating (and optionally recombining) the selected ones
5. Repeat from step 2
```

That's the entire algorithm family in five lines. What changes between different ES variants is *how* you select, *how* you mutate, and *how* you adapt the mutation parameters over time. CMA-ES, the most sophisticated ES, adapts a full covariance matrix that describes the shape of the search distribution. But the loop is the same.

Here's an analogy: imagine you're trying to find the best coffee shop in a new city. You could:

1. **Random search**: Walk into random shops. After 100 tries, pick the best one. (This is surprisingly effective as a baseline, and it's where we start.)
2. **Hill climbing**: Start at one shop. Try the shop next door. If it's better, move there. Repeat. (Fast but gets stuck — what if the best coffee is across town?)
3. **Evolution Strategy**: Start with 10 friends, each at a different shop. Everyone rates their coffee. The friends at the 5 best shops each recommend 2 new shops nearby (with some randomness). Repeat. The "population" of shop-visits quickly converges on the best neighborhoods. And because you have *multiple* searchers, you're much less likely to get stuck.

The genius of ES is that third approach: maintain a *population* of solutions, use the population to estimate where good solutions tend to be, and use that information to generate better candidates.

## 4. Your First ES Optimization

Let's optimize the **Sphere function** — the simplest possible test case:

$$
f(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i^2
$$

The minimum is at the origin (all zeros). Every ES should find this easily. If it can't, something is broken.

### Using pycma (CMA-ES)

```python
import cma
import numpy as np


def sphere(x: np.ndarray) -> float:
    """The sphere function: sum of squared components. Minimum at origin."""
    return float(np.sum(np.square(x)))


# Starting point: random in 10 dimensions
x0 = np.random.randn(10) * 5  # start far from the optimum
sigma0 = 2.0  # initial step size (how far to search)

# Run CMA-ES
es = cma.CMAEvolutionStrategy(x0, sigma0)
es.optimize(sphere)

# Results
result = es.result
print(f"Best fitness: {result.fbest:.2e}")
print(f"Best solution: {np.round(result.xbest, 6)}")
print(f"Function evaluations: {result.evals}")
```

Run this. CMA-ES will find the optimum in roughly 2,000–4,000 function evaluations, depending on how far the starting point is. Not bad for a 10-dimensional search with no gradient information.

### Using Nevergrad

Nevergrad gives you a unified interface to dozens of optimization algorithms:

```python
import nevergrad as ng
import numpy as np


def sphere(x: np.ndarray) -> float:
    return float(np.sum(np.square(x)))


# Define the search space: 10 continuous parameters
parametrization = ng.p.Array(shape=(10,)).set_bounds(-10, 10)

# Try different optimizers
for algo_name in ["CMA", "PSO", "OnePlusOne", "RandomSearch"]:
    optimizer = ng.optimizers.registry[algo_name](
        parametrization=parametrization,
        budget=2000,
    )
    recommendation = optimizer.minimize(sphere)
    print(f"{algo_name:15s} → best fitness: {sphere(recommendation.value):.2e}")
```

This gives you an instant comparison. You'll see CMA-ES crushing the others, RandomSearch doing surprisingly OK (it's hard to fail on a sphere), and PSO and (1+1)-ES somewhere in between.

## 5. Visualizing Population Evolution

The most satisfying thing in ES is *watching* a population converge. Let's plot it:

```python
import cma
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt


def rosenbrock(x: np.ndarray) -> float:
    """The Rosenbrock function: a classic test with a narrow curved valley."""
    return float(np.sum(100 * (x[1:] - x[:-1]**2)**2 + (1 - x[:-1])**2))


# Run CMA-ES on 2D Rosenbrock, collecting populations
x0 = [-2.0, 2.0]
es = cma.CMAEvolutionStrategy(x0, 1.0, {"verbose": -1})

generations = []
while not es.stop():
    solutions = es.ask()
    fitnesses = [rosenbrock(s) for s in solutions]
    es.tell(solutions, fitnesses)
    generations.append(np.array(solutions))

# Plot the landscape
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(8, 6))
xx = np.linspace(-3, 3, 200)
yy = np.linspace(-2, 4, 200)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(xx, yy)
Z = 100 * (Y - X**2)**2 + (1 - X)**2

ax.contourf(X, Y, np.log10(Z + 1), levels=30, cmap="viridis", alpha=0.7)
ax.contour(X, Y, np.log10(Z + 1), levels=30, colors="k", linewidths=0.3, alpha=0.3)

# Plot populations at different generations
colors = plt.cm.coolwarm(np.linspace(0, 1, len(generations)))
for i, (gen, c) in enumerate(zip(generations, colors)):
    if i % max(1, len(generations) // 8) == 0:
        ax.scatter(gen[:, 0], gen[:, 1], c=[c], s=15, alpha=0.6,
                   label=f"Gen {i}", edgecolors="none")

ax.plot(1, 1, "r*", markersize=15, label="Optimum (1,1)")
ax.set_xlabel("$x_1$")
ax.set_ylabel("$x_2$")
ax.set_title("CMA-ES Population Evolving on Rosenbrock")
ax.legend(fontsize=8)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

Watch the cloud of blue dots (early generations, exploring widely) morph into a tight cluster of red dots converging on the optimum at (1, 1). That cloud isn't randomly walking — it's *adapting its shape* to the landscape. CMA-ES learned that the Rosenbrock valley is long and narrow, and it stretched its search distribution to match. No human told it to do that.

## 6. Fitness Landscapes: The Terrain of Optimization

Understanding fitness landscapes is key to understanding why some problems are easy and others are brutal. Let's visualize a few:

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt


def sphere(x: np.ndarray) -> float:
    return float(np.sum(x**2))


def rastrigin(x: np.ndarray) -> float:
    """Rastrigin: sphere + cosine ripples = tons of local optima."""
    n = len(x)
    return float(10 * n + np.sum(x**2 - 10 * np.cos(2 * np.pi * x)))


def rosenbrock(x: np.ndarray) -> float:
    return float(np.sum(100 * (x[1:] - x[:-1]**2)**2 + (1 - x[:-1])**2))


functions = [
    ("Sphere (easy)", sphere, (-5, 5)),
    ("Rastrigin (many traps)", rastrigin, (-5, 5)),
    ("Rosenbrock (narrow valley)", rosenbrock, (-3, 3)),
]

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(15, 4))

for ax, (name, func, bounds) in zip(axes, functions):
    xx = np.linspace(bounds[0], bounds[1], 200)
    X, Y = np.meshgrid(xx, xx)
    Z = np.array([[func(np.array([x, y])) for x in xx] for y in xx])
    ax.contourf(X, Y, np.log10(Z + 1), levels=30, cmap="viridis")
    ax.set_title(name)
    ax.set_xlabel("$x_1$")
    ax.set_ylabel("$x_2$")

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

**Sphere** is a smooth bowl — everything points toward the minimum. Any optimizer can solve this.

**Rastrigin** looks like an egg carton. It has one global minimum but hundreds of local minima that trap greedy methods. Hill climbing would get stuck immediately. ES handles this because it maintains a *population* — even if some individuals fall into traps, others keep exploring.

**Rosenbrock** has a narrow, curved valley. The global minimum is inside the valley at (1, 1), but the valley floor is nearly flat, making it hard to navigate. This is where CMA-ES shines — it learns the shape of the valley.

These three functions are the "Hello World" trio of black-box optimization. You'll see them again and again throughout this course, and in Module 13, we'll benchmark against the full BBOB suite of 24 test functions.[^4]

## 7. ES vs. Gradient Descent: A Head-to-Head

To build intuition, let's compare ES to gradient descent on a problem where both can work:

```python
import numpy as np
import cma


def noisy_sphere(x: np.ndarray) -> float:
    """Sphere function + Gaussian noise. Gradients would be unreliable."""
    return float(np.sum(x**2) + np.random.randn() * 2.0)


# CMA-ES: doesn't care about noise
es = cma.CMAEvolutionStrategy(np.random.randn(5) * 5, 2.0, {"verbose": -1})
es.optimize(noisy_sphere, iterations=200)
print(f"CMA-ES on noisy sphere: best = {es.result.fbest:.4f}")

# Gradient descent: suffers from noise
x = np.random.randn(5) * 5
lr = 0.01
for i in range(2000):
    grad = 2 * x + np.random.randn(5) * 2.0  # noisy gradient
    x = x - lr * grad

print(f"Noisy GD on noisy sphere: final = {np.sum(x**2):.4f}")
```

CMA-ES is a population method — it averages over many evaluations, naturally smoothing out noise. Gradient descent with noisy gradients oscillates and struggles to converge. This is one of the key practical advantages of ES: **robustness to noise**.

## 8. Common Issues & Fixes

| Problem | Solution |
|---------|----------|
| `ModuleNotFoundError: No module named 'cma'` | `pip install cma` |
| CMA-ES runs forever | Check your initial sigma — if it's too small, progress is glacial |
| CMA-ES converges to wrong point | Starting point might be in a local basin; try larger sigma or restart |
| Nevergrad `budget` too low | Most algorithms need at least 100 * dimension evaluations |
| `deap` import errors | `pip install deap` (note: it's `deap`, not `DEAP`) |
| Matplotlib not showing plots | Use `plt.savefig("plot.png")` if running headless |

## 10. Pro Tips from the Trenches

- **CMA-ES is your default.** When someone says "I have a black-box optimization problem," your first answer should be "try CMA-ES." It's robust, self-tuning, and well-understood theoretically.
- **Population size matters.** Too small and you lose diversity; too large and you waste evaluations. CMA-ES has good defaults (roughly $4 + 3 \ln n$ for dimension $n$), but you can increase it for highly multimodal landscapes.
- **Always plot the convergence curve.** It tells you more than the final answer. Is the algorithm still improving? Did it plateau early? Did it diverge? The shape of the curve is diagnostic.
- **Normalize your search space.** If some variables range from 0 to 1 and others from 0 to 10,000, rescale them. ES algorithms treat all dimensions equally — give them dimensions that deserve equal treatment.
- **Run multiple restarts.** A single CMA-ES run can get stuck in a local optimum. IPOP-CMA-ES (Module 05) automates this by increasing the population size with each restart.
